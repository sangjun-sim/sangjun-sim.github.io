## On Death (writing in progress)

*Cautions*. My philosophical views are not sharp, since I am not an expert on philosophy. To be honest, my thoughts are insignificant and lack expertise. Thus, I believe that no one will likely read them. Even so, I must ask questions about death for myself. People may keep apart from this, but I find it more appealing. In this short essay, I would like to question *death*.

*Limitness and non-uniqueness of experience*.
First of all, experience is not novel. The experience one might have during their life has already been had by someone in the past or the future. Thomas Nagel argued in his paper "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (Philosophical Review, 1974) that personal subjective experience cannot be reduced. From his perspective, the phenomenology of our experience is unique. However, the phenomenology cannot simply be the nature of subjective experience. It is because we are not special in the universe, and the experience we had (and will have) occurred thousands of years ago through the same chemical reactions. There are immense things that one has never touched, felt, or seen. Does this limited scope of experience have its own value? I would say, barely not.

*Experience as a chemical reaction*.
Here, the statement that experience is nothing new and consists of a chemical reaction indicates that it is itself a consequence of the chemical reaction. This is because the statement comes from one's brain, where the neurological events take place. It is analogous to eliminative materialism (Paul Churchland, _Matter and Consciousness_ (1984)) and the liar paradox in some sense. In the same way, it can be criticized as follows: Thus, this statement can become meaningless if one says it is true. However, the category of this statement does not lie in that of truth or falsehood. It is actually the combination of two arguments: (i) the experience consists of a chemical reaction, (ii) (We think that) the chemical reaction is nothing new. Even if two arguments are false, the statement becomes true. This is irony and makes one uncomfortable. Rather than that, I suggest this should be a *description* or an axiom of the world.

*Buddhism and us*.
In "Experience as a chemical reaction*", I mentioned that one's cognitive experience is a consequence of a chemical reaction. One cannot change this chemical reaction by oneself. Instead, the environment (surroundings) can. Buddhism explains this by "paticcasamuppāda," which explains the conditional emergence of a state. More specifically, Buddhism teaches that pain or desire is the output of the environment.

Furthermore, Buddhism classifies the desires as: kāma-tanha (desire for pleasure), bhavā-tanha (desire for living), and vibhavā-tanha (desire for nothing). It is popularly recognized that in Buddhism, one should distance oneself from the obsession, kāma-tanha. In contrast, Buddhism states that one must free oneself from these three tanhas. This is similar to Zhuangzi's philosophy: Zhuangzi stated that neither life nor death is a privileged state. Both the desire for life and death are just attachments to the flow of nature. His teaching is to surrender to the flow (non-action). In fact, one can say that he is more genuine in death. For me, their discussions give a clear intuition of life and death.

*Gedanken experiment*.
Imagine an elaborate piece of equipment that measures the chemicals that are relevant to giving people pleasure and pain. From now on, I will name them as H and B materials, respectively. Let us assume we look at the event cycles and concentrations of these chemicals occurring in the brain throughout an individual's lifetime.

I have concluded that there must not be too much pain. In Solomon and Corbit's opponent process theory (1974), all pleasure stimulation leads to the opponent stimulation. Strikingly, it has been known that the opponent stimulation initiates and finishes later than the pleasure stimulation. This was also supported by the research by Baumeister et al. (*Bad is stronger than good*, 2001). In our Gedanken experiment, a large amount of B materials is secreted throughout one's life. This is consistent with "*dukkha*" in Buddhism, which insists that satisfaction is structurally impossible. In addition, since human beings consist of atoms that are fundamental constituents of nature, they follow one of the laws of the universe -- the universe lowers the free energy. To minimize free energy, nature increases entropy or lowers internal energy.

Death increases the entropy. This may not be enough when someone suffers chronically in the present. As a matter of fact, it is known that the critical stress on living things can damage their bodies, which is called allostatic load in biology. Here, nature suggests that he or she should minimize the free energy by reducing the chemical potentials (the number of B materials). It gives four choices: change of the environment, cognitive re-evaluation, physical intervention (exercise or sleep), and shutdown.

In the *Buddhism and us* section, I argued that an individual cannot change their perception on their own. The cognitive re-evaluation is therefore impossible to realize. Now, nature gives three choices: go outside and do exercises, move to another area, or commit suicide. I think these choices are equal and must be treated equally. Here is why. None of these options represents "being free from tanha."

*Aristotle and unpredictability*.
Aristotle said life is the realization of potentiality. And death does end all the possible potentiality, not the **pain**. From the perspective of potentiality, one should ask, "Who judges the potentiality?" The potentiality he mentioned here might be the unpredictability (not the incomputability) of nature. Here, I would like to highlight that unpredictability does not give a reason to live explicitly. It is just there. Just as in the '*experience as a chemical reaction*', the unpredictability is a description.

*Nietzsche's ambiguous standard of life*.
Nietzsche asks whether there is a will to affirm one's life. He also noticed that the utilitarian calculation that "life is worth only when there is more pleasure" is a sign of fragility. However, I had a question about it: Who gives the reason to affirm life? Who knows when to die in freedom? *No one*. His philosophy is silent when one wants a guide to their life, even if it is abstract. Here, I was also looking for any clear grounds or solutions from his qualitative approach alone. However, I could not find it. Instead of that, at least a quantitative utilitarian approach provides "numerical standards." This was more compelling than the ambiguous standard, "quality of will", which is essentially *empty*.

*Thoughts on Wittgenstein*.
Wittgenstein wrote, "death is not an event. Death is not experienced" (_Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus_ 6.4311). I agree with that opinion, but one must go further. An event happens at a specific space and time. Although it is difficult to define a specific moment of death, death happens. Here is the punch line: No matter how he or she did not experience it, death is defined as the objective state with the lowest energy and highest entropy in a closed system like the universe.

*What is the fundamental reason to live?*
During the short research, I could not find any reason to live. This was more important than death. It is because everyone eventually dies. Instead, I came to the decision that life is not that precious, and it is something as disgusting as death. In addition, humans have no will to live, nor do they have a will to die. This is strictly an individual's will and desire, not something that must be carried out because someone (such as a moral or religious authority) gives a command. They are born with no reason and with no will of their own.
